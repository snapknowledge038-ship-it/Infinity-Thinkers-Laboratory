Quasar

What is a Quasar?

A quasar is an Active Galactic Nucleus (AGN). It is not a star, but a supermassive black hole at the center of a young galaxy that is "feeding" on massive amounts of surrounding gas and dust.

As this material falls toward the black hole, it settles into an accretion disk. The extreme gravity and friction heat this material to millions of degrees, causing it to emit incredible amounts of electromagnetic radiation (light, X-rays, and radio waves).

Key Components of a Quasar

  • • Supermassive Black Hole: The central engine, often millions or billions of times more massive than our Sun.
  • • Accretion Disk: A swirling disk of gas and dust spiraling into the black hole. This is the source of the quasar's intense light.
  • • Relativistic Jets: Powerful beams of particles blasted out from the poles of the black hole at nearly the speed of light.
  • • Host Galaxy: The galaxy surrounding the quasar, though it is usually outshined by the quasar's brilliance.

    • Power and Scale

    To understand how powerful a quasar is, consider these facts:
  • • Brightness: A single quasar can be 100 to 1,000 times brighter than the entire Milky Way galaxy (which contains 200–400 billion stars).
  • • Size: Despite their brightness, the central engine of a quasar is relatively small—roughly the size of our solar system.
  • • Distance: Most quasars are found billions of light-years away. Because light takes time to travel, when we look at a quasar, we are actually looking back in time at the early universe.

    • Why don't we see Quasars nearby?

    Quasars are a phase in galactic evolution. They were most common about 10 billion years ago.
  • • In the early universe, galaxies were crowded and had plenty of "fuel" (gas and dust) to feed their central black holes.
  • • As galaxies aged, the fuel was used up or blown away.
  • • The Milky Way likely had a quasar phase in its distant past, but our central black hole (Sagittarius A*) is now "quiet" because it isn't consuming enough material to shine like a quasar.
