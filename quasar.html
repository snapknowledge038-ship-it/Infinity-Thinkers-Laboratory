Quasar

What is a Quasar?

A Quasar is an Active Galactic Nucleus (AGN). It is not a single star, but an extremely powerful light source emitted by dust and gases being consumed by a Supermassive Black Hole at the center of a young galaxy. The friction caused by material being pulled into the black hole heats it to extreme temperatures, emitting more light than an entire galaxy.

Key Components

  • Supermassive Black Hole: The energy hub of the quasar. It contains millions of times the mass of our Sun.
  • Accretion Disk: A rotating disk of superheated gas and dust surrounding the black hole. This disk emits massive amounts of radiation.
  • Relativistic Jets: Powerful streams of particles ejected through the black hole's poles at speeds approaching the speed of light.

Power and Scale

  • Brightness: A single quasar can be about 1,000 times brighter than our entire Milky Way galaxy.
  • Distance: Most quasars exist billions of light-years away. Therefore, when we look at a quasar, we are witnessing events from the early history of the universe.
