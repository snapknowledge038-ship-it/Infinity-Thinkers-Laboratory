Meteor

1. What is a Meteor?

A meteor is a small rocky or metallic body from space that enters Earth's atmosphere. As it travels at high speeds, friction with the air causes it to heat up and glow, creating a streak of light across the sky. These are commonly referred to as "shooting stars" or "falling stars."

2. The Three Stages

Scientists use three different terms depending on where the object is located:
  • Meteoroid: Small chunks of rock or debris still traveling through outer space.
  • Meteor: The streak of light seen when a meteoroid enters the atmosphere and starts burning up.
  • Meteorite: Any part of the object that survives the atmospheric burn and actually lands on Earth's surface.

3. Types of Meteorites

Based on their composition, they are generally classified into three categories:
  1. Stony Meteorites: The most common type, made primarily of silicate minerals.
  2. Iron Meteorites: Composed mostly of iron and nickel; these are very heavy and dense.
  3. Stony-Iron Meteorites: A rare mixture containing both metallic iron and rocky material.

4. Meteor Showers

A meteor shower occurs when Earth passes through a trail of debris left behind by a comet. When these numerous particles enter the atmosphere at once, it creates a spectacular display of many meteors appearing from the same point in the sky.

Examples: The Perseids (visible in August) and the Geminids (visible in December) are two of the most famous annual meteor showers.

5. Why are they Important?

  • Origin of the Solar System: Meteors act as "space fossils," helping scientists understand how the solar system formed billions of years ago.
  • Origin of Life: Some meteorites contain organic compounds like amino acids, leading to theories that the building blocks of life may have been delivered to Earth from space.
