Jack the Ripper

Identity and Origin

"Jack the Ripper" is a pseudonym given to an unidentified killer who terrorized the Whitechapel district in the East End of London in 1888. The name originated from the "Dear Boss" letter sent to the Central News Agency, claiming to be the murderer.

The "Canonical Five" Victims

Historians generally agree on five victims killed by the same hand between August and November 1888:

  • Mary Ann Nichols
  • Annie Chapman
  • Elizabeth Stride
  • Catherine Eddowes
  • Mary Jane Kelly

The Modus Operandi (Method)

The killer's methods were uniquely brutal, gaining him global notoriety:

  • Surgical Precision: The victims' throats were cut, followed by extensive abdominal mutilations.
  • Organ Removal: In several cases, internal organs were removed with professional skill.
  • Expertise: This led investigators to believe the killer had medical training, was a surgeon, or a butcher.

Famous Letters from the Killer

  • "Dear Boss" Letter: Where the name "Jack the Ripper" was first used.
  • "Saucy Jacky" Postcard: Sent shortly after the "Double Event" murders.
  • "From Hell" Letter: Sent with a small box containing half a human kidney preserved in wine.

Why was he never caught?

  • Lack of Technology: There was no DNA profiling, no fingerprinting, and no security cameras in 1888.
  • Environment: Whitechapel was a maze of dark, foggy alleys and overcrowded slums.
  • The Confusion: Mass panic and a lack of organized forensic science made the investigation nearly impossible.
