Jack the Ripper

Identity and Origin

"Jack the Ripper" is a pseudonym (fake name) given to an unidentified killer who terrorized the Whitechapel district in the East End of London in 1888. The name originated from the "Dear Boss" letter sent to the Central News Agency, claiming to be the murderer.

The "Canonical Five" Victims

While many murders occurred in London at the time, historians generally agree on five victims killed by the same hand between August and November 1888:
  • • Mary Ann Nichols
  • • Annie Chapman
  • • Elizabeth Stride
  • • Catherine Eddowes
  • • Mary Jane Kelly

    • The Modus Operandi (Method)

    The killer's methods were uniquely brutal, which is why he gained such notoriety:
  • • Surgical Precision: The victims' throats were cut, followed by extensive abdominal mutilations.
  • • Organ Removal: In several cases, internal organs (such as the kidney or uterus) were removed with great skill.
  • • Expertise: This led investigators to believe the killer had medical training, was a surgeon, or perhaps a butcher.

    • Famous Letters from the Killer

    During the investigation, the police received hundreds of letters. The most famous ones include:
  • • "Dear Boss" Letter: Where the name "Jack the Ripper" was first used.
  • • "Saucy Jacky" Postcard: Sent shortly after two murders occurred on the same night.
  • • "From Hell" Letter: Sent to George Lusk of the Whitechapel Vigilance Committee. This letter arrived with a small box containing half a human kidney, preserved in wine.

    • Why was he never caught?

  • • Lack of Technology: Forensic science was in its infancy. There was no DNA profiling, no fingerprinting, and no security cameras.
  • • Environment: Whitechapel was a maze of dark, foggy alleys and overcrowded slums, making it easy for a killer to vanish.
  • • The "Double Event": On one night, he killed two women in different locations, causing mass confusion for the police.
