Report on the Legendary Sword Excalibur

Excalibur is the legendary sword of King Arthur, the mythical figurehead of British folklore. It is much more than a simple weapon; it serves as a powerful symbol of divine kingship, justice, and the sovereignty of Britain. In many accounts, the sword is described as being so bright that it could blind its enemies, and its name is often translated from Latin and Welsh roots (such as Caledfwlch) to mean "Hard-Cutter."

There is often confusion regarding how Arthur obtained the sword, as mythology offers two distinct versions:
  • The Sword in the Stone: In the earlier versions, Arthur proves his right to the throne by pulling a sword from an anvil sitting atop a stone, which no other man could budge.
  • The Lady of the Lake: In later, more popular romances, Merlin leads Arthur to a mystical lake where a magical hand reaches out of the water to present him with Excalibur. This version emphasizes that the sword belongs to the "Otherworld" and is only lent to the King.

While the blade itself was formidable, Merlin famously told Arthur that the Scabbard (the sheath) was worth ten of the sword. Legend says that as long as Arthur wore the magical scabbard, he would never lose blood or suffer a mortal wound, no matter how many times he was struck in battle. This made the King virtually invincible until the scabbard was eventually stolen by his half-sister, Morgan le Fay.

The story of Excalibur concludes with the tragic Battle of Camlann, where Arthur is mortally wounded. Realizing his time has passed, Arthur orders his last remaining knight, Sir Bedivere, to throw the sword back into the mystical lake. Bedivere hesitates twice, dazzled by the sword’s beauty, but on the third attempt, he throws it. A hand (the Lady of the Lake) rises from the water, catches the sword, brandishes it three times, and vanishes beneath the surface.

While most historians agree that Excalibur is a myth, it represents the transition of Britain from the "Dark Ages" to a structured monarchy. It reflects the Celtic tradition of "votive offerings," where ancient warriors would throw swords into rivers and lakes as gifts to the gods. Even today, Excalibur remains a global cultural icon, representing the ideal of a "True King" who leads with strength and honor.
