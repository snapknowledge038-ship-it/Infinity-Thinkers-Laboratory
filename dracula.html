Dracula

Published in 1897, Bram Stoker’s Dracula stands as the definitive masterpiece of Victorian Gothic fiction. Stoker employs an epistolary structure—a montage of diary entries, telegrams, and newspaper clippings to heighten suspense.

Historical Inspiration

The Count is famously linked to Vlad III (The Impaler), the 15th-century Prince of Wallachia. Stoker transformed this historical figure into an ancient, aristocratic predator with a lineage rooted in Eastern European bloodletting.

The Plot

Following Jonathan Harker’s journey to the Carpathian Mountains, the narrative reveals Dracula’s move to London—the heart of the British Empire. This represents a clash between ancient superstition and modern Victorian rationality.

Abilities and Weaknesses

  • Preternatural Abilities: He can transform into wolves or bats, command fog and storms, and possesses superhuman strength.
  • Arcane Weaknesses: Repelled by garlic and crucifixes, he loses his shapeshifting powers during the daylight.
  • Destruction: Termination requires a wooden stake through the heart, followed by decapitation.

Social Commentary

  • Xenophobia: Represents the fear of an outsider infiltrating and "infecting" London.
  • Scientific Progress: Highlights the tension between medieval folklore and modern science (blood transfusions, phonographs).
  • Repressed Desires: Often analyzed as a metaphor for repressed Victorian sexuality.
