Banshee

Origin and Etymology

The word "Banshee" comes from the Irish Gaelic "Bean Sídhe", which literally translates to "Woman of the Fairy Mound" or "Fairy Woman." In Irish mythology, she is a spirit attached to specific ancient family lineages.

Appearance

According to legends, a Banshee can appear in three different forms:
  • • The Young Woman: A beautiful lady with long, flowing silver or red hair.
  • • The Stately Matron: A middle-aged woman with a more serious or grieving expression.
  • • The Old Hag: A terrifying, withered old woman with sunken eyes and grey hair (this is the most common depiction in modern horror).
    • She is often described wearing a grey hooded cloak or a white burial shroud. Her eyes are usually permanent red from centuries of continuous weeping.

    The Omen of Death (The Keen)

    The most famous characteristic of the Banshee is her piercing scream or wail, known as the "Keen" (caoine).
  • • The Warning: She does not kill people. Instead, her wail is a warning that a member of the household is about to die.
  • • The Sound: Descriptions of the sound vary—some say it is a low, pleasant singing; others describe it as a terrifying screech that can shatter glass.
  • • Timing: The scream is usually heard at night, shortly before a death occurs.

    • The "Five Great Families"

    Ancient tradition suggests that Banshees only followed the five great Milesian families of Ireland: the O'Gradys, O'Neills, O'Briens, O'Connors, and Kavanaghs. However, over time, it was believed she followed any family whose name began with "O" or "Mac".

    Folklore and Superstitions

  • • The Silver Comb: Legend says that if you find a silver comb on the ground in Ireland, you must never pick it up. It is believed to belong to a Banshee, and she will hunt down anyone who takes it.
  • • The Washerwoman: In some parts of Ireland and Scotland, she is seen as the "Bean-Nighe" (the washer), a spirit found near lonely streams washing the blood-stained clothes of those about to die.
