Galaxies

What is a Galaxy?

A Galaxy is a massive system composed of billions of stars, gas clouds, interstellar dust, and Dark Matter, all bound together by gravity. Scientists believe that at the center of almost every galaxy lies a Supermassive Black Hole.

Main Types of Galaxies

  • Spiral Galaxies: Shaped like a flat disk with a central bulge and spiral arms extending outwards (e.g., The Milky Way).
  • Elliptical Galaxies: These range from nearly spherical to elongated egg shapes and contain older stars with very little new star formation.
  • Irregular Galaxies: Galaxies that do not have a distinct regular shape or structure.

Our Home: The Milky Way

  • Shape: It is a large barred spiral galaxy.
  • Size: It has a diameter of approximately 100,000 light-years.
  • Star Count: Estimated to contain between 100 to 400 billion stars.
  • The Center: The heart of our galaxy hosts a supermassive black hole called Sagittarius A*.

Our Neighbors & Collisions

  • Andromeda Galaxy: The nearest major galaxy to us, located about 2.5 million light-years away.
  • Galactic Collision: In about 4.5 billion years, the Milky Way and Andromeda will collide to form a new, giant elliptical galaxy often referred to as "Milkomeda".
Did you know? There are approximately 2 trillion galaxies in the observable universe!
